UQ Assessment Principles

What is the purpose of assessment?

Assessment involves making judgements about the extent to which our students‚Äô work meets identified standards and drawing inferences from these judgements about students‚Äô attainment of learning outcomes. ‚Äã At UQ and across BEL, we use assessment to:‚Äã 

  • engage our students in productive learning;
  • provide feedback to guide improvement;
  • inform teaching and learning decision-making;
  • provide evidence of course- and program-level learning outcomes and graduate attributes;
  • ensure comprehensive, accurate, consistent and trustworthy certification of individual student achievement and program learning outcomes;
  • maintain our professional and disciplinary standards.
  1. Assessment is a developmental learning activity
    Assessment Design In Practice

    ‚óè We engage our students in timely, meaningful learning activities

    ‚óè We help students develop their capacity to make independent judgements about the quality of their own ‚Äì and others‚Äô - work

    ‚óè Forms of assessment are chosen for their suitability in assessing learning outcomes

    ‚óè Minimum weighting of 10% for any assessment item.

    ‚óè 2-4 summative assessment items per course, of which one may be an assessment sequence collectively worth a minimum of 10% or equivalent

    ‚óè With sound pedagogical reasons to do so, a case for >4 assessment items can be made to the BEL-ADA. This could include another sequenced assessment item. 

    ‚óè No marks are awarded for attendance; focus on the quality of performance in relation to the assessment item (e.g., quality of tutorial participation; quality of contribution to group task)  
    Back to top
  2. Assessment involves mutual responsibility
    Assessment Design In Practice

    ‚óè We orient students to assessment methods and standards

    ‚óè We provide timely opportunities for students to demonstrate learning

    ‚óè We support learning progression through effective feedback

    ‚óè We help students understand their responsibility to evidence

    ‚óè Use student-friendly descriptions for all assessment items and submission requirements

    ‚óè Help students understand expectations, including the meaning of words and standards in criteria sheets, so they can more confidently complete assessment and judge own efforts

    ‚óè Provide meaningful and timely feedback (aim for a 2-week turn-around from submission)

    ‚óè Where appropriate, use formative (low stakes) assessment to provide students with early feedback (e.g., by Census date)

    ‚óè Students are provided with the necessary resources and support to manage assessment situations (e.g., working in groups; knowing how to create a required digital artefact)

    ‚óè Peer review is required for group/team assessment items*

    *Variance to this action can only be made in consultation with the Program Convenor
    Back to top
  3. Assessment is criterion‚Äëreferenced
    Assessment Design In Practice

    ‚óè We judge the quality of each student‚Äôs performance against explicit and predetermined criteria and standards (i.e., not against the achievement of other students)

    ‚óè Criteria sheets are accessible by students at semester start (i.e., before end of Week 1)

    ‚óè Criteria and standards are specified and explained for each assessment item

    ‚óè Criteria sheets reflect, and support, the task students are being asked to complete

    ‚óè Assessment provides the opportunity for a range of competencies in learning, i.e., from minimum to highest standards (e.g., 1-7 scale or equivalent)
    Back to top
  4. Assessment is transparent
    Assessment Design In Practice

    ‚óè There is an explicit and logical relationship between the assessment item, criteria used as the basis of assessment judgement, and the grades associated with standards of performance

    ‚óè Each assessment item is progressively moderated, ensuring consistency of judgements across the cohort in relation to set criteria and standards

    ‚óè Ideally, 2 markers assess performance/presentation items together; where not possible, performance/presentation items are recorded [NOTE: Items >20% should be recorded]

    ‚óè Final grades are awarded in line with the UQ Grading System 

    ‚óè Hurdles are rarely used and must serve a genuine pedagogical purpose. Consultation with the Program Convenor and approval by the DoTL is required for hurdle decisions. 
    Back to top
  5. Assessment is valid
    Assessment Design In Practice

    ‚óè Our assessment reliably measures intended learning outcomes, providing a dependable indication of the quality of students‚Äô performance

    ‚óè Our assessment items achieve consistent, fair, reliable and valid results

    ‚óè Assessment items generate valid evidence of learning

    ‚óè Assigned weightings reflect the importance of the learning outcome/s being assessed

    ‚óè Learning outcomes are not over-assessed (e.g., the same 5 LOs are not assessed in all course assessment items)

    ‚óè Assessment is of sufficient standard to enable students to demonstrate they deserve the grade awarded 

    ‚óè Use of short response items, such as MCQs in exams/quizzes, is limited to 25% of overall course assessment with these items used less frequently, if at all, in later years of study

    ‚óè Assessment items do not enable the memorisation or reproduction of answers to previous assessment items to result in a passing grade
    Back to top
  6. Assessment is authentic
    Assessment Design In Practice

    ‚óè Our assessment items provide appropriate, meaningful, significant and worthwhile forms of accomplishment for students

    ‚óè Assessment activities authentically reflect intended learning outcome/s

    ‚óè Where possible, assessment items require students to demonstrate the knowledge and/or skills used by practitioners in real-life settings

    ‚óè Group assessment reflects an authentic need for students to work in groups/teams to manage and complete the task

    Back to top
  7. Assessment is fair and inclusive
    Assessment Design In Practice

    ‚óè We respect and accommodate the diverse needs of learners, enabling them to interact with and respond to learning outcomes

    ‚óè We support students‚Äô equitable engagement with the content, format and conduct of assessment to ensure no-one is unfairly advantaged or disadvantaged

    ‚óè Forms of assessment are varied across the Program and within courses

    ‚óè No single form of assessment (including ‚Äòlike‚Äô items such as exams, quizzes and tests) is used to assess all learning outcomes in a course

    ‚óè No assessment item, including assessment sequences but excluding project-based assessments and theses, contribute more than 60% to the final grade

    ‚óè The timing of high stakes assessment items is carefully considered, ensuring students have progressive feedback opportunities to address learning gaps

    ‚óè Students know how to apply for extensions
    Back to top
  8. Assessment is a purposeful, professional activity
    Assessment Design In Practice

    ‚óè We understand that assessment is integral to course and program design, review and evaluation

    ‚óè We ensure there is quality assurance and moderation of assessment at program- and course-levels, and within schools and faculties

    ‚óè We collaborate with colleagues to provide whole-of-program approaches that make assessment a coherent experience for students

    ‚óè We commit to ongoing revision and enhancement of our assessment practices

    ‚óè Course Coordinators liaise with Program Convenors to ensure learning activities and assessment items reflect the intended course/program experience for students

    ‚óè Each assessment item is progressively monitored and moderated, ensuring equity of student experience and consistency of grading judgements across the cohort

    ‚óè At semester‚Äôs end, Course Coordinators submit course reflections to Program Convenors, providing evidence of the student experience which supports evidence-informed decisions about the quality, validity and fairness of assessment  
    Back to top
  9. Assessment is sustainable and workable
    Assessment Design In Practice

    ‚óè We resource assessment in ways that are achievable for teachers and students with reasonable effort

    ‚óè Assessment is designed per the availability and appropriate allocation of resources, and in relation to the intended student experience

    ‚óè Additional resources may be directed to courses where new or innovative approaches are being piloted

    ‚óè Word/time limits^ reflect the expectations and weighting of the assessment item

    ‚óè No School-based exams or in-class tests are scheduled during the final 2 weeks of semester

    ‚óè No assessment is held or due during a revision period (see UQ Academic Calendar) 

    ^If available, see School guidance on word/time limits, noting this is a guide (not UQ Policy) and that limits can vary depending on the nature of the task. Your school or faculty Learning Designer can help guide your decision making.  
    Back to top
  10. Assessment is secure
    Assessment Design In Practice

    ‚óè We provide a verified measure of student performance at program level

    ‚óè We help students learn how to uphold the value of integrity in their studies and assessment responses

    ‚óè We help students learn how to maintain ethical standards

    ‚óè In designing and conducting various forms of secure assessment, we give due regard to assessment integrity to minimise the possibility of undetected academic misconduct

    ‚óè At least one assessment item in each course is secure, with a minimum weighting of 30% 

    ‚óè Secure assessment is easily identifiable at course and program levels  

    ‚óè Secure assessment requires identity verification, e.g., in person seminars/presentations, individual/group projects actively monitored by teaching staff, interactive oral assessment, invigilated tests/exams, thesis project with active monitoring and recording of student progress  

    ‚óè See Assessment Security Guidelines and AI Teacher Hub

Secure Assessment

Ensuring academic integrity and authentic student performance

UQ Policy

‚ÄòSecure assessment' is one of ten assessment principles outlined in UQ‚Äôs Assessment Policy Secure assessment:

  • is designed to ensure academic integrity and prevent misconduct,
  • is supervised including, but not restricted to, invigilation,
  • may restrict the use of generative Artificial Intelligence (genAI),
  • is intended to confirm that students have undertaken the work and learning themselves, and
  • must account for at least 30% of the overall course grade or act as a Pass/Fail hurdle.

    •  

    Every course must include at least one secure assessment item. The following is broad guidance to increase assessment integrity and security, and offer assurance that assessment tasks are completed by the student who receives credit for them.

 

Secure Assessment Strategies

  • Secure Questioning: Student discussions about submitted work in supervised conditions
  • Scaffolded Submission: Samples of work developed in supervised settings
  • Real‚Äëtime Completion: Entire tasks completed under supervision
  • Viva Voce: Oral examination components
  • Corroboration: Work verified by trusted observers
  • Interactive oral assessments - Students respond to structured or semi-structured questions in real-time scenario-based about a topic area that in an unscripted conversation interaction. See Question and prompt guide for interactive oral assessments.
  • Interactive and in-person simulations/ task demonstration - A scenario, role-play, demonstration in which students individually or in groups demonstrate a skill or speak to/apply knowledge from the course in real-time, under supervised conditions.
  • Oral defence (of report, essay, presentation, project design, code, etc.) - Students demonstrate understanding of concepts of work completed unobserved by answering spontaneous questions orally, in-person, in real-time, without access to technology. This can be run in a number of settings, including sequence as regular check-ins of semester-long project.
  • Thesis/group/studio projects - Teaching staff actively monitor and document/record student and or team progress over the semester.
  • In-person examination - invigilated written or oral response to questions or stimuli that have not been seen in advance.

    • Question and prompt guide for interactive oral assessments (IOA)

Purpose of Hurdles

Hurdles exist to ensure quality and integrity in our programs, but they should not create unnecessary barriers. Hurdles are used sparingly, determined on sound pedagogical grounds. By using hurdles only where necessary, communicating clearly, and supporting students to meet them, we can maintain high standards and foster positive student experiences. 

 

By using hurdles only where necessary, communicating clearly, and supporting students to meet them, we can maintain high standards and foster positive student experiences. 

  1. Key Principle #1
    Assessment Design Key Principle #1

    Use hurdles only when needed

    Critical Competency and Security: add a hurdle only when essential for the learning outcomes, e.g., where you need to securely assure a learning outcome or a professional competency that every student must demonstrate to pass.

    Where feasible, draw on program assessment planning to identify where hurdles are critical. NOTE: If standard/existing assessments can ensure students meet the outcome, a hurdle might not be needed.

    ¬†
    Back to top
  2. Key Principle #2
    Key Principle #2 In Practice

    Ensure hurdle clarity

    Clarity in the Course Profile: prominently display hurdle requirements and consequences in the course profile. From Week 1 of the semester, clearly explain to students why the hurdle exists. Students must know what the hurdle is and the reason for it up front.

     

    Communicate early and often: remind students during the semester about the hurdle and their progress. Be explicit about what happens if they don‚Äôt meet the hurdle. No student should be surprised by a hurdle at the end of semester ‚Äì transparency reduces anxiety and builds trust.
    Back to top
  3. Key Principle #3
    Key Principle #3 In Practice

    Support students to succeed on hurdles

    Set students up to succeed: provide practice, review sessions, and/or formative quizzes on the hurdle‚Äôs content. Emphasise that the hurdle isn‚Äôt meant to catch students out but ensure they have achieved important outcomes.

     

    Second chances where possible: particularly for in-semester hurdles, offer second attempt when feasible. For instance, a timely re-sit or alternative task gives students an opportunity to demonstrate competence without delaying their progress.
    Back to top

    2. Reasonable Standards: Set achievable thresholds that demonstrate competency without unnecessary stress

    3. Clear Communication: Explicitly state hurdle requirements, rationale, and consequences in course profiles from day one

    Open Assessment (AI‚ÄëPermitted)

    Students can use AI tools transparently with proper acknowledgment. Design considerations:

    • Set clear expectations about AI acknowledgment requirements in course profile
    • Model effective AI use through scaffolded prompts and examples
    • Use Microsoft Copilot to simulate AI's role in completing tasks
    • Design tasks requiring synthesis, application, and critical thinking beyond AI capabilities
    • Consider tasks that ask students to evaluate, critique, or improve AI‚Äëgenerated content
